Fig. II — Procedure

How a small intelligence
looks at a cow.

PashuAI does one thing — it identifies Indian cow breeds. It can't write essays or play chess. But it knows cows better than most people will in a lifetime. Here's how it works, step by step.
i.
A photograph enters the network

A clear lateral view in daylight is best. JPG or PNG, up to 10MB. The image is processed and discarded — never written to disk.

ii.
Fifty layers read the animal

Convolutional layers extract hump curvature, dewlap shape, horn arc, coat micro-texture and body silhouette — features even seasoned vets weigh by feel. The network has learned to see what we look at.

iii.
A breed name, with reasons

A ranked list of probable breeds with confidence, lineage, regional origin, and a curated set of diseases the breed is statistically prone to. Grad-CAM heatmaps explain why the model decided what it did.

Technology, plainly.

01 / NEURAL ARCHITECTURE
ResNet-50 with transfer learning

Fifty-layer residual network pre-trained on ImageNet, then fine-tuned for 42 epochs on 14,000+ Indian cattle photographs. Skip-connections allow effective gradient flow despite the network's depth.

02 / SERVING STACK
Flask backend, Pythonic and lean

A modest Flask REST API handles uploads, PIL preprocessing (resize · normalize), ResNet inference, and structured JSON responses. Sub-second latency on commodity CPU hardware.

03 / EXPLAINABILITY
Grad-CAM, so the model can show its work

Gradient-weighted Class Activation Maps highlight which regions of the image drove the prediction — useful for non-expert users who want to verify that the model is looking at the animal, not the background.

04 / CLIENT
A web frontend designed for the field

Lightweight HTML/CSS/JS with drag-and-drop upload, animated confidence visualisation, breed disease insights, and downloadable plain-text reports. Works on weak connections, any device.

Performance, honestly.

— TRAINING DYNAMICS
Loss curve convergence

Training loss decreases from ~1.15 to below 0.15 across epochs, indicating strong feature convergence. Validation loss stabilises early thanks to data augmentation and dropout regularisation.

— KNOWN LIMITATIONS
Where the model still stumbles

Sahiwal and Lakhimi share similar reddish-brown coat patterns; the model occasionally confuses them — a well-known challenge in fine-grained breed classification. Future work: expand the dataset and add multi-view input.

THE PROBLEM WE SOLVE

India has 50+ cow breeds.
Most farmers can't tell them apart.

A trained vet can. But India has too few vets, and they live in cities. Out in the villages — where 70% of India's cattle actually live — nobody knows for sure what breed is in front of them. That ignorance is expensive.

01

Farmers lose money

A pure Sahiwal cow is worth ₹80,000. A crossbred one is worth ₹30,000. Most farmers can't prove which they own, so they sell at the lower price. They also miss government breed-specific schemes worth thousands of rupees per year.

02

Native breeds are vanishing

Indian breeds are disease-resistant and handle heat well. But they're being replaced by foreign breeds that produce more milk in the short term. Without breed records, the government can't run conservation schemes — and once a breed is gone, it's gone forever.

03

Care goes wrong

Different breeds get different diseases. Sahiwal cattle are prone to mastitis. Gir cattle to tick fever. If the farmer doesn't know the breed, the vet doesn't know what to watch for. Treatment becomes guesswork — and animals suffer.
